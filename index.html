What Is Cluster Headache?

Key facts, epidemiology, and the current research landscape

⚡ The Basics

  • Pain: Excruciating unilateral orbital, supraorbital, or temporal pain lasting 15–180 minutes — often called "suicide headache"
  • Autonomic features: Tearing, red eye, drooping eyelid, nasal congestion, sweating — all on the pain side
  • Pattern: 1–8 attacks/day, frequently occurring during sleep, clustering in periods of weeks to months
  • Types: Episodic (cycles with remission) vs. Chronic (no remission >3 months)
  • Prevalence: ~0.1% of population; 3–4× more common in men historically, though the gender gap is narrowing
  • Key pathways: Hypothalamus (circadian timing) + trigeminal-autonomic reflex (pain generation)

🔬 Current Research Focus Areas

  • Psychedelics: 2 Phase 2 LSD RCTs actively recruiting; psilocybin trials publishing results
  • CGRP pathway: Galcanezumab approved for episodic CH; others showed mixed results
  • Sleep-hypothalamus axis: SUNCET trial testing sodium oxybate for nocturnal attacks in chronic CH
  • Neuromodulation: New implantable combined trigeminal+occipital stimulator (PRIMUS)
  • Genetics: 8 loci identified in latest GWAS studies (2024–2025)
  • Biomarkers: MicroRNA profiling underway at IRCCS Mondino, Italy

💊 What's Currently Approved

  • Acute: High-flow O₂ (most effective), SC sumatriptan 6mg, intranasal zolmitriptan 5mg
  • Preventive: Verapamil (first-line), lithium, galcanezumab (Emgality — episodic only)
  • Transitional: Greater occipital nerve block, short prednisone course
  • Device: gammaCore (FDA-cleared nVNS) for episodic acute & chronic prevention

Clinical Treatments

Evidence-based treatments — approved drugs, devices, and procedural options

⚡ Acute Abortives

🫁 High-Flow Oxygen

First-line ✓
  • 12–25 L/min via non-rebreather (NRB) mask
  • 78% efficacy in pivotal RCT (Cohen 2009, BMJ) at 12 L/min; higher flows often more effective
  • 15–25 min duration or until resolved
  • Hyperventilation technique speeds abort
  • Demand-valve mask: fastest community method

💉 Sumatriptan SC 6mg

First-line ✓
  • Onset ~10 minutes; most reliable pharma abortive
  • Subcutaneous injection only (oral too slow)
  • Intranasal zolmitriptan 5mg as alternative
  • Triptans block serotonergic treatments (see Community tab for details)
  • Limit use to avoid medication overuse

🔌 gammaCore (nVNS)

FDA-cleared
  • Non-invasive vagus nerve stimulator (neck device)
  • FDA-cleared: acute episodic + prevention chronic CH
  • ACT1/ACT2 trials: superior to sham for episodic CH
  • PREVA trial: superior to standard care alone (chronic)
  • Can be used at home, no prescription for device

💊 Civamide (Zucapsaicin)

Phase 3 completed
  • Synthetic capsaicin analogue, intranasal
  • Phase 3 trials showed efficacy for episodic CH
  • Development stalled; not commercially available
  • Depletes substance P from trigeminal fibers

🛡️ Preventive Treatments

💊 Verapamil

First-line ✓
  • Most widely used preventive; onset 2–3 weeks
  • Effective dose: 360–960 mg/day (divided doses)
  • ECG monitoring required at higher doses (AV block risk)
  • Active cardiac safety trial: NCT04406259 (Nice, FR)
  • Bridge with GON block or prednisone while starting

💊 Galcanezumab (Emgality)

FDA-approved ✓
  • Only anti-CGRP mAb approved for CH (episodic only)
  • Monthly 300mg injection; onset within days
  • Did not meet primary endpoints in chronic CH trials
  • Fremanezumab & erenumab: negative in CH trials
  • Eptinezumab: mixed results, some benefit at week 4

💊 Lithium

Second-line
  • Effective for chronic CH, especially refractory
  • Narrow therapeutic window (0.6–1.2 mEq/L)
  • Regular blood monitoring required
  • Consider with verapamil under specialist supervision

💊 Melatonin (10–25mg)

Adjunct option
  • RCT: 50% responder rate at 10mg at bedtime (n=20)
  • Particularly effective for nocturnal attacks
  • Excellent tolerability; OTC in most countries
  • 10mg is the RCT-studied dose; doses above this are empirical

💉 GON Block

Transitional ✓
  • Corticosteroid + local anesthetic at skull base
  • Onset 24–72h; duration 2–8 weeks
  • Use as bridge while verapamil takes effect
  • Phase 3 trial ongoing: NCT05324748 (Leiden)
  • Also: repeated GON blocks being studied as prevention

🧠 SPG Stimulation

Refractory CH
  • Implantable sphenopalatine ganglion neurostimulator
  • On-demand acute relief; strong evidence base
  • For refractory cases when medications fail
  • Pulsed radiofrequency (PRF) non-implant alternative in trials (NCT06787677)

🔬 In Active Clinical Trials

🍄 Psilocybin

Phase 2
  • Repeat pulse reduces attacks by 50% (2024 RCT)
  • Australian MRFF-funded trial underway (2025)
  • Health Canada: first-ever legal access for CH (2024)
  • Sub-hallucinogenic doses appear sufficient

🔬 LSD (Micro/Low Dose)

Phase 2
  • Basel RCT: 3×100µg, double-blind crossover (NCT03781128)
  • Radboud RCT: minidosing for chronic CH (NCT05477459)
  • Both recruiting; results expected 2025–2027
  • Community use ("busting") for 20+ years

💉 Ketamine + Magnesium IV

Phase 4 (KETALGIA)

Phase 4 designation reflects ketamine’s approved status for other indications (anesthesia), not for CH.

  • Single IV infusion for refractory chronic CH
  • Pilot: 13/17 patients ≥50% attack reduction
  • 14 sites in France; 90 patients (NCT04814381)
  • Primary completion Sep 2026

😴 Sodium Oxybate (SUNCET)

Phase 2
  • Targets nocturnal CH via sleep deepening (↑SWS, ↓REM)
  • RCT at Leiden University (NCT06950281)
  • n=52; starts Jan 2026; results expected Dec 2027
  • Wearable sleep monitoring included

☀️ Light Therapy

Proof of concept
  • Luminettes® device for chronic CH prevention
  • Hypothesis: circadian/hypothalamic reset
  • n=48, Marseille (NCT06540651); ends Feb 2027

💉 Botulinum Toxin A (SPG)

Phase 3
  • SPG blockade with BoNT-A for refractory chronic CH
  • 5 European sites; 112 patients (NCT03944876)
  • Norwegian University of Science & Technology
  • Primary completion Sep 2025

🍄 The Busting Protocol (Clusterbusters)

"Busting" is the patient-community term for using psychedelic substances to break CH cycles. Popularized and formally organized by Bob Wold, who founded Clusterbusters after his own experience. Now being evaluated in Phase 2 clinical trials.

Substances used
  • Psilocybin mushrooms (most common): 0.5–1.5g dried (sub-psychedelic) or 1.5–3.5g
  • LSD: micro to low dose
  • LSA seeds (legal): Rivea corymbosa (50–100 seeds) or Hawaiian Baby Woodrose (1–2 seeds)
Protocol
  • Dose every 5 days (prevents serotonin tolerance)
  • Typical course: 3 doses; many report cycle breaking after 1–2
  • CRITICAL: Stop triptans 5+ days before. SSRIs/SNRIs require much longer washout (2–5 weeks depending on drug) — consult your doctor before stopping any antidepressant
  • Join Clusterbusters forums before attempting

☀️ Vitamin D3 Anti-Inflammatory Regimen (Batch Protocol)

Developed by Pete Batcheller (CH sufferer, retired Navy pilot). Survey of 110 patients: 80% reported significant reduction in frequency, duration, and severity. Formal clinical trial NCT04570475 ongoing.

Core supplement
  • Vitamin D3: 10,000 IU/day starting dose
  • Some titrate to 20,000–50,000 IU based on blood tests
  • Takes 2–4 weeks to show effect
Essential cofactors (do not skip)
  • Vitamin K2 (MK-7): 100–200 mcg/day
  • Magnesium glycinate/malate: 400–800 mg/day
  • Omega-3 fish oil: 3–6g/day
  • Boron: 3 mg/day; Zinc: 15–25 mg/day
  • Vitamin A (retinol): 5,000 IU/day

Full protocol: vitamindregimen.com

Triggers & Avoidance

Community-identified triggers — especially relevant during active cluster cycles

TriggerCommunity NotesSeverity
Alcohol (any type)Even a small amount reliably triggers attacks during an active bout. The community universally avoids all alcohol during cycles.High ⚠️
Altitude / Low oxygenFlights, mountains, high altitude environments. Some patients pre-treat with supplemental O₂ before flying. Directly relevant to CH's O₂ mechanism.High ⚠️
Strong chemical smellsGasoline, paint fumes, perfume, cleaning products, solvents. Wear a mask if exposure is unavoidable. Common workplace trigger.High ⚠️
Sleep disruptionCH attacks frequently occur during or around REM sleep transitions. Jet lag, shift work, naps, or any disruption to sleep schedule reliably worsen cycles for many. Direct hypothalamic mechanism.High ⚠️
Vasodilating drugsNitrates (GTN sprays), sildenafil/tadalafil, some blood pressure medications. Always inform prescribers about CH diagnosis.High ⚠️
Histamine-rich foodsAged cheeses, cured/processed meats, fermented foods. Not universal but frequently reported, especially during active cycles. (Red wine is a double trigger — both histamine and alcohol/vasodilation.)Medium
Heat exposureHot baths, saunas, intense exercise in heat. Triggers for some; others find hot showers offer temporary relief during an attack. Individual variation.Medium
Bright / flickering lightLess consistent than migraine triggers but reported by a subset of patients, especially during attacks.Low-Medium
Season changesMany episodic CH patients have predictable seasonal patterns (spring, autumn). Not avoidable, but useful to anticipate and start preventives early.Predictive
Stress / relaxation after stress"Let-down" headache — attacks often occur after stress resolves (weekends, vacations). Classic pattern in episodic patients.Variable

✅ During an Active Cycle

  • Zero alcohol — even one drink can trigger an attack within 30–60 minutes
  • Maintain strict sleep schedule; protect sleep timing
  • Carry O₂ or sumatriptan injection at all times
  • Inform airline staff / travel with O₂ documentation
  • Avoid scented products and chemical environments
  • Pre-treat with O₂ before known trigger exposure where possible
  • Keep a headache diary to identify personal triggers
  • Alert family/colleagues — attacks are very visible and frightening
